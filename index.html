ABOUT LUMI

In recent years, digital companions, ranging from virtual assistants to humanoid robots, have emerged as integral tools across domains such as healthcare, education, customer service, and emotional support. As their presence becomes more commonplace, the critical question is not just how well these companions perform tasks, but how much users trust and accept them. This project, titled “Enhancing Human Trust and Acceptance of Digital Companions through Anthropomorphic Design”, addresses this very need by investigating how human-like features can foster deeper user trust and engagement.

The core objective is to design and integrate anthropomorphic traits into digital companions across four key domains:

  1. Visual Design and Appearance
  2. Emotional Expressions
  3. Social Behaviors and Gestures
  4. Voice-Based Communication

Each domain contributes uniquely to the digital companion’s ability to build rapport, express empathy, and resonate culturally with users. Together, they form a unified system that mirrors human behavior and social norms, thereby enhancing trust, relatability, and long-term user acceptance.

Rise of Digital Companions in Society The adoption of digital companions is rapidly growing, driven by advancements in artificial intelligence (AI), natural language processing (NLP), and real-time 3D animation technologies. Systems like Siri, Alexa, and Replika are early examples of AI-driven assistants that support basic interactions. However, the limitation of these systems lies in their lack of emotional intelligence, adaptability, and cultural awareness, all of which are vital for building trust.

Trust and Anthropomorphism Human beings naturally anthropomorphize objects, attributing human traits, emotions, and intentions to non-human agents. This phenomenon plays a vital role in how people perceive and interact with AI. Research by Fong et al. (2003) and more recent work by de Visser et al. (2016) show that digital companions with human-like expressions and behavior are perceived as more competent, trustworthy, and empathetic. In practical terms, a smiling avatar with appropriate eye contact can elicit greater user engagement than a static, machine-like interface. This anthropomorphic approach not only enhances realism but also creates the illusion of empathy, which is a powerful trust building mechanism.

Emotional Intelligence and Cultural Fit Emotionally intelligent systems respond dynamically to user emotions through facial expressions, tone of voice, and gestures. These responses are interpreted through psychological frameworks such as the Valence-Arousal-Dominance (VAD) model. VAD helps classify emotional states on axes of pleasure, activation, and control, allowing for granular and context-aware reactions.

However, cultural variation in emotion expression is often overlooked. A study by Dissanayake et al. (2020) found that Sri Lankan users tend to understate negative emotions in formal contexts, which might be misread by Western-trained models. Integrating culturally adaptive emotion recognition is crucial for global usability.

As digital companions evolve from novelty assistants to emotionally aware and socially integrated entities, the need for systems that can foster user trust has become increasingly urgent. While task completion and linguistic intelligence are foundational to such systems, these alone are not sufficient to build meaningful, long-term engagement with human users. Instead, trust in artificial companions arises from the perception that the system is socially competent, emotionally aware, and culturally sensitive. The absence of these human-centric qualities in most digital companions today reveals a critical research problem at the intersection of artificial intelligence, psychology, and human-computer interaction.

Trust in human-technology interaction is inherently tied to the user’s belief that the system understands them, emotionally, socially, and contextually. Studies show that users are more inclined to engage with digital companions that respond empathetically and behave appropriately according to cultural expectations. For example, a smile or a nod from an avatar at the right moment can make the interaction feel genuine, while an inappropriate or exaggerated response can create discomfort or even lead to user disengagement.

The core research problem identified in this study is that most existing digital companions lack a cohesive, multimodal architecture capable of interpreting user emotion, behavior, and context, and of responding with synchronized emotional expressions, speech, and gestures in a culturally appropriate manner.

Despite the proliferation of conversational AI and embodied digital companions across sectors, most existing systems fall short in supporting trust-building mechanisms rooted in emotional, cultural, and behavioral adaptability. This gap is especially evident in systems deployed in emotionally sensitive domains like mental health, education, and eldercare, where interpersonal resonance is essential.

Commercially successful digital assistants such as Siri, Alexa, and Google Assistant have demonstrated functional proficiency in executing tasks through voice commands. However, they lack the ability to perceive user emotions or contextually adapt their responses. These assistants function largely through static dialogue flows and do not exhibit any significant form of empathetic or anthropomorphic behavior. Even more socially designed applications, such as Replika or Woebot, although emotionally expressive in text, do not feature real-time, multimodal responsiveness that includes gesture, facial expressions, and culturally sensitive visual or verbal cues.

Recent academic literature has identified that users' willingness to engage meaningfully with AI systems is strongly tied to their perception of being understood and respected, both emotionally and culturally. For instance, Brandt and Hazel emphasized that most current systems are shaped by Western-centric communication norms, which often create dissonance when deployed in non-Western cultures. This lack of inclusivity in design may lead to alienation, especially in populations with nuanced communication styles like those in South Asia or the Middle East.

Furthermore, although emotion recognition technology has made significant strides using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and large labeled datasets such as FER-2013, its application in real-world digital companion systems remains limited. These models often function in isolation and are not integrated with avatar interfaces or speech systems in real time. There is also an overreliance on static facial data, ignoring body gestures and non verbal emotional cues, which are crucial components of human interaction.

A particular void lies in the absence of dynamic gesture-driven systems. As noted by Jithma et al. in their component of this study, gestures such as waving, leaning, or crossing arms carry critical social meaning that varies across cultures. However, current digital companions are unable to interpret these behaviors contextually and respond appropriately. The failure to integrate gesture recognition with avatar behavior inhibits the system's ability to simulate authentic human interactions.

To address the complex and multifaceted research problem identified earlier, this study establishes a set of structured objectives designed to guide the development of a culturally adaptive, emotionally intelligent, and multimodally expressive digital companion. These objectives are both theoretical and practical in nature, spanning system design, technical implementation, and empirical evaluation. Together, they ensure that the research is methodologically grounded and results oriented. The ultimate goal is to develop a digital companion that goes beyond functional assistance and becomes a socially aware, emotionally responsive entity that users can trust and relate to. This companion should be able to perceive user behavior, emotions, and preferences in real time, and generate responses that are culturally congruent, behaviorally expressive, and emotionally appropriate.

General Objective

To design and develop a real-time, culturally adaptive digital companion system that integrates facial emotion recognition, body gesture analysis, voice communication, and anthropomorphic visual expression, in order to enhance human trust and acceptance. This overarching objective is rooted in the interdisciplinary goal of advancing human computer interaction (HCI) by embedding emotional intelligence, cultural understanding, and visual realism into the core architecture of AI-driven digital companions.

Specific Objectives

The general aim is operationalized through the following specific objectives, which correspond to the four key pillars of the system: emotion recognition, gesture interpretation, visual appearance, and voice communication.

  • 1. To implement a real-time facial emotion recognition system

    This objective involves building a deep learning model capable of classifying facial expressions into discrete emotional states (e.g., happiness, sadness, anger, surprise). The model is trained using a robust facial expression dataset (FER-2013) and deployed using optimized architectures like MobileNetV2 to ensure fast and accurate inference on web platforms. The system also includes post-processing modules to overlay cultural interpretation rules that refine emotion labels based on the user’s regional context.


  • 2. To develop a culturally adaptive visual appearance framework

    Digital companions often adopt a generic visual style that does not reflect the user’s demographic identity or cultural background. This objective focuses on personalizing the avatar's appearance using user attributes such as age, gender, and regional identity. The system employs a machine learning classification model that predicts these traits from webcam input and dynamically loads appropriate 3D avatars with culturally relevant clothing, skin tones, and facial features using platforms like ReadyPlayerMe and Blender.


  • 2. To develop a culturally adaptive visual appearance framework

    Beyond facial expressions, this objective targets full-body gesture interpretation using computer vision techniques such as skeletal tracking with MediaPipe. A convolutional neural network (CNN) classifies body postures and movement patterns (e.g., waving, slouching, walking) and maps these to social behaviors and emotional states using a VAD (Valence-Arousal-Dominance) framework. The avatar then mirrors these interpretations through synchronized body gestures and animations, adding depth to the interaction.


  • 4. To implement an emotionally expressive and culturally sensitive voice communication system framework

    This objective involves building a pipeline for voice-based interaction that detects emotional tone from user speech, generates appropriate verbal responses using a language model (e.g., ChatGPT), and delivers those responses via expressive text to-speech synthesis (e.g., ElevenLabs). The system also includes a lip-sync engine using Rhubarb to ensure the avatar’s mouth movements align with the audio output. Furthermore, the voice tone, vocabulary, and speaking style are modulated to align with the user’s cultural expectations.


  • 5. To synchronize multimodal outputs into a coherent avatar interface

    A key challenge in creating lifelike digital companions is ensuring that visual (facial and body gestures), verbal (voice tone and words), and emotional (recognized state) outputs are temporally aligned. This objective focuses on integrating all subsystems through a synchronization layer that manages response timing and animation sequences to create seamless, real-time interactions.


  • 6. To evaluate the system's impact on trust, acceptance, and user experience

    Finally, the research includes an empirical evaluation of the system using controlled experiments with diverse user groups. Metrics include perceived trustworthiness, emotional resonance, cultural appropriateness, and usability. Both quantitative (Likert-scale questionnaires) and qualitative (interviews, observations) methods are employed to assess the effectiveness of the companion in real-world scenarios.

The methodology adopted in this study integrates multiple domains—computer vision, natural language processing, affective computing, 3D graphics, and cultural modeling— into a modular, synchronized system for creating emotionally intelligent and culturally adaptive digital companions. The development process followed a design science research (DSR) framework, characterized by iterative prototyping, testing, and refinement of core components.

This chapter details the systematic steps followed in developing each component of the system and their eventual integration into a unified digital companion framework.
